Medical Contributions

The Concept of Molecular Disease

Pauling originated the concept of molecular disease, which revolutionized the understanding of genetic disorders. In 1945, after hearing about sickle cell anemia, Pauling hypothesized that the disease was caused by a defect in the hemoglobin molecule. His research, in collaboration with Dr. Harvey Itano, identified sickle cell anemia as a genetically transmitted molecular disorder, where deoxygenated hemoglobin molecules stick together, forming rods that interfere with blood circulation. This was the first disease identified as a result of a molecular defect. Paulingâ€™s discovery led to further research on other molecular diseases, and it laid the foundation for modern genetics, including research in the human genome.

Orthomolecular Medicine

Paulingâ€™s later work in orthomolecular medicine focused on the role of vitamins and other nutrients in maintaining health and treating diseases. He believed that health problems could often be treated by correcting deficiencies in biochemical substances naturally occurring in the body. He founded orthomolecular psychiatry in 1968, which proposed using vitamins and micronutrients to treat mental illness, especially schizophrenia, instead of relying on synthetic drugs. Pauling expanded this concept to orthomolecular medicine, which advocates for the use of the right molecules in the right concentrations for the prevention and treatment of disease.

Vitamin C Research

One of Pauling's most publicized contributions was his work on vitamin C. He became fascinated with its role in maintaining health and began advocating for high-dose vitamin C as a treatment for the common cold. In 1970, he published Vitamin C and the Common Cold, which popularized the idea that large doses of vitamin C could prevent or alleviate colds. Later, Pauling extended his research to show that vitamin C could also be beneficial for cardiovascular diseases, cancer, and degenerative conditions in aging.
