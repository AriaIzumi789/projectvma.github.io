Linus Pauling: A Pioneer of Science and Peace

Scientist, Nobel Laureate, and Peace Advocate

Linus Pauling was one of the most influential scientists of the 20th century, known for his groundbreaking contributions to both chemistry and global peace. Awarded two Nobel Prizes—one in Chemistry for his work on chemical bonding, and the other in Peace for his efforts in nuclear disarmament—Pauling’s legacy spans beyond the laboratory. His discoveries in molecular biology, including the alpha-helix structure of proteins, revolutionized our understanding of life at a molecular level. A passionate advocate for world peace, he played a pivotal role in the campaign against nuclear weapons testing and was recognized for his advocacy with the Nobel Peace Prize in 1962.

Quick Facts:

  • Born: February 28, 1901
  • Died: August 19, 1994
  • Nobel Prizes: Chemistry (1954), Peace (1962)
  • Known for: Chemical bonding theory, Vitamin C research, Nuclear disarmament activism

Key Events in Pauling's Life

1901

Linus Pauling is born on February 28 in Portland, Oregon, USA.

1922

Graduates from Oregon State University with a degree in Chemical Engineering. He begins his studies in chemistry, which will shape the rest of his career.

1925

Begins graduate studies at California Institute of Technology (Caltech), where he works with leading scientists like Robert A. Millikan.

1927

Earns his PhD from Caltech and starts conducting groundbreaking research on chemical bonding.

1931

Appointed as a professor at Caltech, where he continues to develop his theories on the nature of chemical bonds.

1954

Wins the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for his work on the nature of the chemical bond.

1962

Awarded the Nobel Peace Prize for his advocacy against nuclear weapons testing and his efforts in promoting disarmament.

1994

Linus Pauling passes away on August 19 at the age of 93 in Big Sur, California.

Philosophy of the Scientist

"The purpose of life is to contribute in some way to making things better."

This quote reflects Pauling’s belief in the responsibility of individuals, especially scientists, to use their knowledge and skills for the greater good. For Pauling, science was never just about discovery—it was a tool for improving humanity, whether through advancing medicine, improving public health, or advocating for global peace.
